Step 0A — Market type
Market type classification
Before any indicator is checked, spend at least 10 minutes observing the 1-minute chart with full concentration. The market type determines which signals to act on and which to ignore.
What to look for
Big player — smooth, sustained directional moves with deep, meaningful retracements. Strong conviction. Trends last days or weeks.
Scalper — rapid oscillations, surges that fade quickly, low commitment. Price reverses frequently with little follow-through.
Institutional — sudden outsized moves, event-driven, fakeouts common. Not always sustainable. Avoid chasing.
Magnet — price drifts back toward a centre level. Surges exhaust. No conviction in either direction.
Step 0B — Fundamentals
Fundamental analysis
Currencies trade in pairs — always assess both sides. Fundamentals explain why a market type has formed. Expected rate decisions are already priced in; what moves markets is deviation from expectation.
What to look for
Strong fundamentals + conviction = Big Player market. Trade with the stronger currency.
Major event upcoming — reduce position size or stand aside until after the release.
Conflicting fundamentals = no clear bias. Do not force a direction — wait for clarity.
Write down key drivers for both currencies before every trade — this forces clarity and discipline.
Rule 1 — 50 + 200 moving averages
Moving averages (50 MA + 200 MA)
Two simple moving averages on the daily chart. The 50 MA tracks medium-term trend; the 200 MA tracks long-term trend. Both must align before a trade is considered.
What to look for
Price above BOTH MAs + 50 MA above 200 MA = strong bullish trend. Only consider buy setups.
Price below BOTH MAs + 50 MA below 200 MA = strong bearish trend. Only consider sell setups.
Price between the two MAs = conflicting signal. No trade — stand aside until price clearly breaks one way.
MAs close together = weak trend. Wait for separation before committing.
Rule 2 — Bollinger bands (20 / 2.0)
Bollinger bands
Three bands around price: a 20-period moving average (middle), and upper/lower bands 2 standard deviations away. The bands expand in high volatility and contract in low volatility. The signal to use depends on the market type.
What to look for
Squeeze (bands narrowing) = coiling energy, big move coming. Watch for direction — do not enter during squeeze itself.
Breakout signal (Big Player market) — daily candle closes above upper band (buy) or below lower band (sell).
Fade signal (Scalper/Magnet market) — price touches band then closes back inside. Trade the reversal.
Price colliding into lower band in an uptrend = potential bounce. Confirm with RSI and S&R before acting.
Price mid-range between bands = no signal. Wait for a band extreme.
Rule 3 — RSI (14)
Relative strength index (RSI 14)
An oscillator measuring momentum strength on a scale of 0–100. Above 70 = overbought. Below 30 = oversold. The key signal in this framework is divergence — when price and RSI disagree.
What to look for
Bullish divergence — price makes a lower low but RSI makes a higher low. Hidden buying momentum. Strong buy signal.
Bearish divergence — price makes a higher high but RSI makes a lower high. Momentum secretly fading. Strong sell signal.
RSI above 70 in a buy setup = overbought risk. Wait for RSI to reset toward 50.
RSI below 30 in a sell setup = oversold risk. Bounce likely before continuation.
RSI between 45–55 = neutral momentum. Wait for a reading to develop.
Rule 3 — MACD (12 / 26 / 9)
MACD (moving average convergence divergence)
Shows the relationship between two moving averages. Consists of a histogram (bars) and a signal line (dotted). The histogram shows momentum strength. Both RSI and MACD must confirm before entry.
What to look for
Histogram crossing above zero = bullish crossover signal. Strong buy confirmation when aligned with RSI and trend.
Histogram above zero and growing = strong bullish momentum building.
Histogram crossing below zero = bearish crossover signal.
Histogram above zero but shrinking = bullish momentum fading. Do not enter new longs.
Histogram below zero but shrinking = bearish momentum fading. Watch for bullish crossover.
Flat histogram near zero = no momentum signal. Wait for a clear cross.
Rule 4 — Support & resistance
Support & resistance (key daily/weekly closes)
Levels defined by where price has repeatedly closed on daily and weekly charts. Only trade at hard zone extremes — never in the middle of a range.
What to look for
Resistance turned support — a previously resistant level now acting as support. One of the strongest hard zone signals.
Touch and hold (Big Player market) — price touches the S&R level, confirmation candle closes back on the correct side.
False breakout return (Institutional market) — price briefly pierces the level then closes back inside. Trade the return.
Soft zone — unstable area just beyond support/resistance. Avoid entering here.
Mid-range — price between key levels. Do not trade here. Wait for a hard zone extreme.
Exit parameters
Stop loss, take profit & position sizing
Exit rules are defined before entry — never after. The stop loss defines your risk. Position size is calculated from your risk tolerance, not from how much you want to make.
What to look for
Stop loss — place 50–75 pips beyond the S&R level on the other side.
Target 1 — close 50% of position at 1:2 risk/reward. Locks in profit regardless of what the remainder does.
Target 2 — run the remainder to the next major S&R level. Move stop to breakeven once Target 1 is hit.
Time stop — if no directional move within 30 minutes of entry candle close, reassess and consider exiting.
Never risk more than 1–2% of account equity on a single trade.
